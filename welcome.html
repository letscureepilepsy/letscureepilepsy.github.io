Facts about Epilepsy

- An estimated 65 million people worldwide currently live with epilepsy.

-One in 26 Americans will develop epilepsy in their lifetime.

-Epilepsy is the fourth most common neurological disorder in the United States after migraine, stroke, and Alzheimer's disease. About one percent of Americans have some form of epilepsy, and nearly four percent (1 in 26) will develop epilepsy at some point in their lives.

-Epilepsy can develop in any person at any age, but, as of late, especially in children around the age of one.

-Almost 150,000 people in the U.S. develope epilepsy EVERY YEAR.

-In definition, epilepsy is a neurological disorder marked by sudden recurrent episodes of sensory disturbance, loss of consciousness, or convulsions, associated with abnormal electrical activity in the brain. Or, in simpler terms, epilepsy is a disorder in which nerve cell activity in the brain is disturbed which causes seizures.

-It is estimated that up to 50,000 deaths occur annually in the U.S. from status epilepticus (prolonged seizures), Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy (SUDEP), and other seizure-related causes such as drowning and other accidents. Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy (SUDEP) accounts for 34% of all sudden deaths in children.

-And one of the facts that I found most shocking was the following: although epilepsy is among the most common neurological disorders and its associated economic costs are high, federal funding in epilepsy is MODEST and LAGS behind other common neurological conditions. Put it into Perspective

I remember watching the video where Miguel and Kelly Cervantes told their story. In the video, Kelly Cervantes said something along the lines of, "Living with epilepsy isn't something we want to do, but it is something we HAVE to do." Hearing this line was like receiving a punch in the gut...I was indescribably shocked and even more saddened on behalf of those who have to suffer and endure as much pain and hardship as the Cervantes family does. And, as if this line wasn't heartbreaking enough, Miguel Cervantes said next, with tears in his eyes, "I don't feel like I have ever met her. I look at her and just pray that she will look at me and smile. And if there is a possibility that I may not get that, may never get to see that, and no one can help me find that...it makes me very sad." Families living with epilepsy, they don't have a choice.
